The Adaptability Framework

A leadership framework for the AI transition, anchored in 3,000 years of reading the land.

Most organizations say they will adapt to AI. That claim is more arrogant than it sounds. The more consequential question is not how to deploy AI effectively. It is whether your leadership has developed the capacity to remain genuinely adaptable as the ground shifts beneath the deployment.

Adaptability as a Business Discipline

Adaptation and adaptability are not the same thing.

To adapt is to assume you know what is coming — to bet on a single path and specialize for it. To be adapted to a situation is to be specialized for it. To be adaptable is to be polyvalent — to invest in multiple paths simultaneously, to maintain enough range that even weak points hold under pressure when conditions shift. The first is a posture of confidence about a future no one can see. The second is a posture of readiness.

The environment is becoming more uncertain, not less. An organization that adapts to a future it cannot know has frozen itself into a rigid choice. The biology is instructive: robust living systems are not perfectly adapted — they are adaptable. Plants that appear standardized maintain highly heterogeneous cells beneath the surface. Living things multiply plan Bs to avoid the fragility of over-specialization. What looks like redundancy is the mechanism of survival.

Organizations have mostly built themselves around the opposite principle. Performance culture, optimization metrics, and roadmap discipline have trained leaders to execute against predicted conditions. That worked when markets changed on quarterly cycles. It does not work when AI is rewriting the cost structure of knowledge work in real time and the next state is genuinely unknown.

As machine intelligence absorbs the optimization layer of work — the measurable, repeatable, efficient outputs — what remains uniquely human is the adaptability layer: holding multiple paths open, reading signals before they resolve, and acting before the full picture is clear. That capacity does not develop in conference rooms. It develops in environments that demand it.

The distinction between adaptation and adaptability draws on Olivier Hamant’s work in plant biology and organizational resilience.
What the Land Actually Teaches

Continuous readers of a living system.

The Ohlone Aramai people lived on the coastal headlands of what is now Pacifica, California for over 3,000 years. They were not static inhabitants. They were continuous readers of a living system — coastal fog, creek flows, animal behavior, seasonal cycles — and every decision they made was calibrated to what the environment was actually doing, not what a model predicted it should do.

Their cosmology centered exactly this practice. Coyote, Eagle, and Hummingbird were not symbols. They were the named archetypes of adaptable intelligence: the opportunist who thrives at the margins, the elevated observer who sees the whole system, and the unlikely survivor whose persistence is disproportionate to apparent fragility. The Aramai read these animals, and by extension the entire landscape, as a continuous source of strategic information.

That practice — sustained attention to a living system as a signal source — is the cognitive discipline the AI transition demands. The Signs Framework turns each encounter in that landscape into a direct question about the participant’s active business context.

The Signs Framework

Ten signs. Ten questions the land has been asking for 3,000 years.

Each sign is anchored in Aramai cosmology or the documented history of the Pacifica coast and the Skyline ridge. Each carries a direct question for the executive in AI transition. What actually surfaces in a field session depends on what the land chooses to show on the day of the walk.

01

Coyote

The trickster-creator. Thrives in margins, degraded zones, and disrupted ecosystems. Teaches through flawed improvisation, not perfection.

Where in your organization is value being created at the margins — outside the sanctioned processes? Are you protecting those zones or optimizing them away?

02

Red-tailed Hawk

Eagle’s role: elevated perspective, authority, the ability to see what others cannot from ground level. Mediator between realms.

When did you last gain altitude on your own operation? What signal are you missing because you are inside the system rather than reading it from above?

03

Hummingbird

The unlikely survivor. Stood with Eagle and Coyote at the beginning of the world. Persistence, precision, and disproportionate resilience relative to size.

What small, apparently fragile capability in your organization carries disproportionate adaptability? Are you starving it?

04

Garter Snake

Lishumsha — a trail at Mori Point bears this name. Moves between water and land, between ecosystems. Thrives on edges.

Where are the boundary crossings in your business — between functions, markets, or customer segments — and who is actually navigating them effectively?

05

Brown Pelican

Once functionally extinct from DDT; now recovered. A species that held enough redundancy to return from the edge of loss.

What has your organization nearly lost that is now worth deliberately recovering? What systemic condition almost killed it, and is that condition gone?

06

Arroyo Willow

Framework of Aramai dwellings, pain relief from bark, basketry material. A single plant serving structure, medicine, and craft.

What single capability in your organization is quietly serving three or four different functions? Are you naming it, protecting it, or taking it for granted?

07

Coastal Fog

Moves daily across Pacifica — ecosystem-sustaining, visibility-limiting, and impossible to resist. Shaped Aramai decisions about movement and timing.

What recurring ambiguity in your operating environment are you trying to eliminate rather than learn to navigate? What does timing look like when fog is a constant, not an exception?

08

Calera Creek

The water source anchoring Timigtac for 3,000 years. A single flowing system that determined the entire village’s location and survival.

What is the actual flowing resource your business is organized around — not your stated mission, but the real energy source? Is it as reliable as you assume?

09

Gray Whale

Passes Pacifica twice yearly on a 10,000-mile migration the Aramai witnessed for 3,000 years. Once hunted to near extinction; whalers called gray whales the most intelligent species because they learned to reroute around threats. Recovered and still visible from Mori Point.

What long-cycle movement is happening around your business that you are too zoomed in to see? When the threat becomes clear, will your organization be capable of rerouting at scale — or is it too committed to its current path to change course?

10

Methuselah Redwood

1,800 years old, 14-foot diameter, ridgetop on Skyline Boulevard. Spared by 19th-century loggers because its burls and knobs made it commercially unworkable. The trees with cleaner grain — perfectly adapted to the logging economy of the day — were cut. Methuselah’s redundancies were its plan Bs. It did not adapt to one future. It remained adaptable across many.

What about your organization looks like a flaw to the current market but may be the exact feature that carries you through a paradigm shift? What are you being told to smooth out that you should instead be protecting?

Who This Is For

The capability gap is not technical. It is attentional.

Leaders responsible for AI workforce strategy who sense that standard consulting frameworks — workforce planning models, skills taxonomies, change management playbooks — are not adequate to the actual complexity of what they are navigating. The human question in AI transformation is not a talent problem. It is a perception problem. The field session is where that capacity awakens.
