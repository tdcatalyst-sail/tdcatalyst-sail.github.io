The Adaptation Method

Five frameworks that hold under AI workforce change.

Each framework draws from a named body of research. Each has been tested inside real programs with real revenue and real stakes.

01

Signs Framework

AI does not arrive in a single announcement. It arrives as weak signals that precede structural workforce change: a tool that quietly replaces a first task, a team whose scope shifts without the org chart moving, a hiring plan that slows without being cancelled. The Signs Framework is a practice of reading those signals before they harden into decisions.

It asks three questions of any observed change. What is this a sign of. What would confirm or deny it within 60 days. What is the smallest move that keeps options open.

Original to TDcatalyst. Refined across 40 plus enterprise and public sector engagements.

02

Dynamic Capabilities

Teece’s framework separates three distinct organizational muscles: sensing new opportunities and threats, seizing them through resource commitment, and reconfiguring assets as conditions change. AI transformation fails most often at the third muscle. Organizations that sense and seize without reconfiguring end up with AI pilots that never make it into operating rhythm.

Inside the method, dynamic capabilities map directly to the leadership decisions that cannot be outsourced: what to sense, who decides to commit, what gets dismantled to make room.

Teece, D. J. (2007). Explicating Dynamic Capabilities. Strategic Management Journal. UC Berkeley.
03

Organizational Ambidexterity

O’Reilly and Tushman document how the companies that survive technology transitions run two operating modes at once: exploitation of the current business and exploration of the next one. AI workforce transformation is an ambidexterity problem. The team running production cannot also be the team redesigning production, and the company cannot afford to pick only one.

The method treats this as a structural question first, cultural second. Where does exploration sit. How is it funded. Who has the authority to kill an experiment. Who has the authority to protect one.

O’Reilly, C. A. (Stanford) and Tushman, M. L. (Harvard). The Ambidextrous Organization. Harvard Business Review.
04

Sensing and Responding Loop

Burnard and Bhamra study how organizations close the gap between detecting a signal and acting on it. Under AI change the gap is often measured in quarters when it should be measured in weeks. Their loop isolates where delay accumulates: in escalation paths, in review cadence, in the absence of a pre approved response.

The method borrows their diagnostic and adds a practical cut. For any given AI signal, the organization must name the response before the signal arrives, or it will not respond in time.

Burnard, K. and Bhamra, R. Organisational Resilience: Development of a Conceptual Framework for Organisational Responses. International Journal of Production Research.
05

The Methuselah Principle

Methuselah is a redwood estimated at 1,800 years old. It has survived through adaptation in place rather than replacement. Its defenses are thick bark, a high crown that lifts out of most fire lines, and a root system that shares resources with neighbors. None of this is clever. All of it is patient.

Applied to AI workforce transformation, the principle argues against the urge to rebuild the organization from scratch at every technology turn. The durable pattern is adaptation in place, funded over years, with clear rules for what gets shed and what gets reinforced. The method treats this as the long time horizon that every other framework sits inside.

Named after Methuselah, a coastal redwood estimated at roughly 1,800 years, standing in the Muir Woods region of California.
